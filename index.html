Microeconomics

Supply & Demand

The Law of Demand

As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship), ceteris paribus. The demand curve slopes downward due to two effects:

  • Substitution effect: As the price of a good rises, consumers substitute cheaper alternatives
  • Income effect: A higher price reduces real purchasing power, so consumers buy less
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional unit consumed provides less satisfaction, so consumers only buy more at lower prices

Demand Shifters — TRIBE (shift the entire curve, not just movement along it):

  • Tastes & preferences — e.g., a health trend increases demand for organic food
  • Related goods — if complement price rises, demand falls; if substitute price rises, demand increases
  • Income — normal goods: demand rises with income; inferior goods: demand falls
  • Buyers — more consumers in a market shifts demand right
  • Expectations — if consumers expect higher future prices, they buy more now (shifts right)
Key distinction: A change in price causes a movement along the demand curve (change in quantity demanded). A change in any other factor shifts the curve (change in demand).

The Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases (positive relationship). The supply curve slopes upward because higher prices make production more profitable, incentivizing more output.

Supply Shifters — ROTTEN:

  • Resource/input costs — rising wages or raw material costs shift supply left (decrease supply)
  • Other goods' prices — if a related good becomes more profitable to produce, supply of this good falls
  • Technology — improvements lower production costs, shift supply right
  • Taxes & subsidies — taxes increase costs (shift left); subsidies decrease costs (shift right)
  • Expectations — if sellers expect higher future prices, they may withhold supply now
  • Number of sellers — more producers = greater market supply (shift right)

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where Qd = Qs — the market-clearing price. At this point, there is no tendency for price to change.

  • Surplus (excess supply): Price above equilibrium → Qs > Qd → downward pressure on price
  • Shortage (excess demand): Price below equilibrium → Qd > Qs → upward pressure on price
Supply & Demand — Market Equilibrium
Equilibrium Shift Rules
D↑ (right)→ P* rises, Q* rises
D↓ (left)→ P* falls, Q* falls
S↑ (right)→ P* falls, Q* rises
S↓ (left)→ P* rises, Q* falls
Both shiftOne of P* or Q* becomes indeterminate without magnitudes

Consumer & Producer Surplus

  • Consumer surplus: The difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Area above price, below demand curve.
  • Producer surplus: The difference between the price producers receive and their minimum acceptable price. Area below price, above supply curve.
  • Total surplus (social welfare): Consumer surplus + Producer surplus. Maximized at competitive equilibrium.
  • Deadweight loss: Loss of total surplus when output deviates from equilibrium quantity (caused by price controls, taxes, monopoly)
Consumer & Producer Surplus

Price Controls

  • Price ceiling: A legally imposed maximum price set below equilibrium. Creates a persistent shortage. Example: rent control.
  • Price floor: A legally imposed minimum price set above equilibrium. Creates a persistent surplus. Example: minimum wage.
Both price controls create deadweight loss and reduce market efficiency. Ceilings → shortages. Floors → surpluses.

Taxes & Subsidies (Wedge Analysis)

  • A per-unit tax drives a wedge between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive. Quantity falls, deadweight loss is created.
  • Tax incidence: Who actually bears the burden depends on elasticity — the less elastic side bears more of the tax burden.
  • A subsidy works in reverse — lowers the price buyers pay, raises seller revenue, increases quantity above equilibrium.
Tax burden on buyers = (Price buyers pay) − (Original equilibrium price)
Tax burden on sellers = (Original equilibrium price) − (Price sellers receive)
Microeconomics

Elasticity

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. Always use absolute value.

Price Elasticity of Demand
PED= |% ΔQd ÷ % ΔP|
Midpoint= [(Q₂−Q₁)/((Q₁+Q₂)/2)] ÷ [(P₂−P₁)/((P₁+P₂)/2)]
  • |PED| > 1 → Elastic: consumers are sensitive to price
  • |PED| < 1 → Inelastic: consumers are insensitive to price
  • |PED| = 1 → Unit elastic
  • |PED| = 0 → Perfectly inelastic: vertical demand curve
  • |PED| = ∞ → Perfectly elastic: horizontal demand curve
Elastic vs. Inelastic Demand Curves

Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand

  • Availability of substitutes: More substitutes = more elastic
  • Necessity vs. luxury: Necessities tend to be inelastic; luxuries tend to be elastic
  • Time horizon: Demand becomes more elastic over time
  • Proportion of income: Large share of income = more elastic
  • Narrowness of definition: "Food" is inelastic; "McDonald's hamburgers" is elastic

Elasticity & Total Revenue

Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity. Elasticity determines how TR responds to a price change:

  • Elastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↓
  • Inelastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↑
  • Unit elastic: Price change has no effect on TR
This is why drug companies can raise prices (inelastic) and still make more revenue, but airlines increase revenue by lowering prices (elastic).

Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

Price Elasticity of Supply
PES= % ΔQs ÷ % ΔP
PES > 1Elastic — producers ramp output easily
PES < 1Inelastic — hard to increase output quickly

Income & Cross-Price Elasticity

Income & Cross-Price Elasticity
YED= % ΔDemand ÷ % ΔIncome
XED= % ΔDemand for Good A ÷ % ΔPrice of Good B
YED > 0Normal good
YED < 0Inferior good
XED > 0Substitutes
XED < 0Complements

Market Structures

Perfect Competition

  • Many buyers and sellers; no single firm influences price
  • Identical (homogeneous) products
  • Perfect information for all participants
  • Free entry and exit in the long run
  • Firms are price takers — perfectly elastic demand curve
  • Long run: Zero economic profit (but normal/accounting profit)
  • Produces at allocative efficiency (P = MC) and productive efficiency (P = min ATC)
Profit Maximization Rule (All Firms)
All firmsProduce where MR = MC
Perf. Comp.P = MR, so produce where P = MC
Economic π= (P − ATC) × Q

Monopoly

  • Single seller — the firm IS the market
  • Unique product with no close substitutes
  • Significant barriers to entry
  • Price maker — MR < P
  • Results in deadweight loss
  • NOT allocatively or productively efficient: P > MC and P > min ATC
Monopoly — Profit Maximization & Deadweight Loss
Price discrimination: A monopolist charging different prices to different customers can capture more consumer surplus and reduce deadweight loss.

Monopolistic Competition

  • Many firms selling differentiated products
  • Low barriers to entry and exit
  • Some pricing power (slight downward-sloping demand)
  • Long run: Zero economic profit. Excess capacity.
  • Examples: restaurants, clothing brands, hair salons

Oligopoly

  • Few large firms dominate; high barriers to entry
  • Interdependence: Each firm's decisions affect competitors
  • Collusion & cartels: Firms may fix prices (illegal in the US)
  • Game theory: Prisoner's dilemma explains why cartels break down
  • Examples: airlines, major tech firms, auto manufacturers

Market Structure Comparison

Market Structure Comparison
Perf. Comp.Many firms | Homogeneous | Price taker | P=MC | Zero LR profit
Mono. Comp.Many firms | Differentiated | Some power | P>MC | Zero LR profit
OligopolyFew firms | Diff/Homo | Price maker | P>MC | Possible LR profit
MonopolyOne firm | Unique | Price maker | P>MC | Possible LR profit
Microeconomics

Consumer Theory & Market Failures

Utility & Marginal Analysis

Utility is the satisfaction a consumer receives from consuming a good or service.

  • Total Utility (TU): Total satisfaction from all units consumed
  • Marginal Utility (MU): Additional satisfaction from one more unit. MU = ΔTU / ΔQ
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: As consumption increases, each additional unit provides less additional utility.
Utility-maximizing rule: MU₁/P₁ = MU₂/P₂ (allocate spending so last dollar spent on each good gives equal marginal utility)

Costs of Production

  • Explicit costs: Direct monetary payments
  • Implicit costs: Opportunity costs of using owned resources
  • Economic profit = TR − (Explicit + Implicit costs)
  • Accounting profit = TR − Explicit costs only
  • Normal profit: Zero economic profit
Cost Formulas
TC= FC + VC
MC= ΔTC ÷ ΔQ
ATC= TC ÷ Q
AFC= FC ÷ Q (falls as Q rises)
AVC= VC ÷ Q
Econ. π= TR − (Explicit + Implicit costs)
The MC curve intersects ATC and AVC at their minimum points. When MC < ATC, ATC is falling. When MC > ATC, ATC is rising.

Returns to Scale & Economies of Scale

  • Economies of scale: As output increases, long-run ATC falls
  • Diseconomies of scale: Output grows too large, long-run ATC rises
  • Constant returns to scale: Long-run ATC stays flat
  • Natural monopoly: One firm serves the entire market at lower cost due to large economies of scale

Externalities & Market Failures

A market failure occurs when the free market does not allocate resources efficiently.

  • Negative externality: Cost on third party. Market overproduces. Fix: Pigouvian tax.
  • Positive externality: Benefit to third party. Market underproduces. Fix: subsidy.
  • Public goods: Non-excludable and non-rival. Free-rider problem.
  • Asymmetric information: One party has more info (lemons problem, adverse selection).
Externality Rules
Social opt.MSB = MSC
Neg. ext.MPC < MSC → overproduces → tax
Pos. ext.MPB < MSB → underproduces → subsidy
Macroeconomics

GDP & Economic Growth

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period.

  • Only final goods counted — avoid double counting
  • Only new production — resale excluded
  • Only goods produced within the country
  • Transfer payments NOT included
GDP Expenditure Approach
GDP= C + I + G + (X − M)
CConsumer spending (~70% of US GDP)
IGross private investment
GGovernment spending (NOT transfers)
X − MNet exports

Real vs. Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current year prices
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation using a base year
  • GDP Deflator: Broad price index for conversion
Real GDP & Growth Rate
Real GDP= (Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator) × 100
Growth %= [(Real GDP₂ − Real GDP₁) ÷ Real GDP₁] × 100
If nominal GDP grows 5% but inflation is 3%, real GDP only grew 2%. Always use real GDP to compare over time.

Business Cycle

  • Expansion: Rising real GDP, falling unemployment
  • Peak: Maximum output; inflation pressure
  • Contraction/Recession: Falling real GDP for 2+ quarters
  • Trough: Lowest point of output
  • Recovery: GDP begins rising again
The Business Cycle
Leading indicators predict future activity (stock prices, building permits). Lagging indicators confirm trends (unemployment rate, CPI).

AD-AS Model

  • Aggregate Demand (AD): Total spending (C+I+G+NX). Slopes downward.
  • SRAS: Slopes upward — higher prices incentivize more production when input prices fixed.
  • LRAS: Vertical at full-employment output.
  • Output gap: Actual vs potential GDP.
AD-AS Model — Short Run & Long Run
AD-AS Shift Rules
AD rightC↑, I↑, G↑, NX↑ → higher output + higher prices (SR)
SRAS leftInput costs rise → stagflation
LRAS rightMore resources / better tech → long-run growth

Recessionary & Inflationary Gaps

  • Recessionary gap: Actual < potential. Fix: expansionary policy.
  • Inflationary gap: Actual > potential. Fix: contractionary policy.
  • Long run: wages adjust → SRAS shifts → economy self-corrects (slowly).
Macroeconomics

Unemployment

Types of Unemployment

  • Frictional: Transitioning between jobs. Normal and healthy.
  • Structural: Skills mismatch. Hardest to fix — requires retraining.
  • Cyclical: Caused by economic downturns. Targeted by policy.
  • Seasonal: Predictable time-of-year changes.
Full employment ≠ 0% unemployment. It means cyclical unemployment = 0.

Measuring Unemployment

Unemployment Formulas
U-rate= (Unemployed ÷ Labor Force) × 100
Labor Force= Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking)
LFPR= (Labor Force ÷ Working-Age Pop.) × 100
  • Discouraged workers: Not counted — understates true unemployment
  • Underemployment: Also not captured in headline rate
  • BLS: U-3 (official) through U-6 (broadest)

NAIRU

Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment = frictional + structural. Typically ~4–5% for the US.

Phillips Curve

  • Short-run: Inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation
  • Long-run: Vertical at natural rate — no trade-off
  • Stagflation (1970s) broke the short-run relationship
Short-Run vs. Long-Run Phillips Curve
Macroeconomics

Inflation

What is Inflation?

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level, eroding purchasing power.

Inflation Formulas
Inflation= [(CPI₂ − CPI₁) ÷ CPI₁] × 100
CPI= (Cost of basket current ÷ Cost base year) × 100
  • CPI: Most common inflation measure
  • PCE: Fed's preferred measure — broader
  • Core inflation: Excludes food and energy
  • PPI: Seller's perspective — leading indicator

Causes of Inflation

  • Demand-pull: AD rises faster than capacity. "Too much money chasing too few goods."
  • Cost-push: Supply shock raises costs. SRAS left → stagflation.
  • Built-in / Wage-price spiral: Self-fulfilling inflation expectations.
  • Quantity theory: MV = PQ. If M grows faster than Q, P rises.

Effects of Inflation

  • Hurts creditors and fixed-income earners. Helps debtors.
  • Menu costs: Resources wasted updating prices
  • Shoe-leather costs: More trips to bank during high inflation
Fisher Equation
Real Rate= Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

Deflation & Hyperinflation

  • Deflation: Falling prices → consumers delay purchases → recession deepens
  • Disinflation: Inflation positive but slowing
  • Hyperinflation: >50%/month. Destroys currency value.
  • Fed targets 2% inflation
Macroeconomics

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve

  • Central bank of the US, established 1913. Independent.
  • FOMC sets policy — meets 8 times per year
  • Dual mandate: Maximum employment + stable prices (2% target)
  • Chair: Jerome Powell (term expires May 2026)

Tools of Monetary Policy

  • Federal funds rate: Primary tool. Banks lend reserves overnight.
  • Open Market Operations: Buy bonds → money supply ↑ → rates ↓. Sell bonds → opposite.
  • Discount rate: Rate Fed charges banks for direct borrowing.
  • Reserve requirement: Set to 0% since 2020.
  • Interest on Reserves (IOR): Controls the floor of fed funds rate.

Expansionary vs. Contractionary

Monetary Policy Transmission
Expansionary↓ Fed rate → ↓ interest rates → ↑ I & C → ↑ AD → ↑ GDP, ↓ unemployment
Contractionary↑ Fed rate → ↑ interest rates → ↓ I & C → ↓ AD → ↓ inflation

Money Supply & Multiplier

Money Multiplier
Multiplier= 1 ÷ Reserve Requirement
ΔMoney Supply= Initial Deposit × Money Multiplier
Example10% reserve → multiplier = 10 → $1,000 → $10,000
QE: When rates hit near-zero, the Fed buys longer-term assets to push down long-term rates.

Lags

  • Recognition lag: Time to identify problems
  • Implementation lag: Fast — FOMC can act at any meeting
  • Impact lag: 6–18 months for full effect
Macroeconomics

Fiscal Policy

What is Fiscal Policy?

Government use of taxation and spending to influence the economy. Set by Congress and the President.

Fiscal Policy Effects on AD
ExpansionaryG↑ or T↓ → AD shifts right → GDP↑, unemployment↓
ContractionaryG↓ or T↑ → AD shifts left → inflation↓

The Spending Multiplier

Spending Multiplier
Multiplier= 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ MPS
MPCMarginal Propensity to Consume
MPSMarginal Propensity to Save
IdentityMPC + MPS = 1
ExampleMPC=0.8 → Mult=5 → $100B = $500B GDP impact
Tax mult.= −MPC ÷ MPS (smaller than spending multiplier)
The higher the MPC, the larger the multiplier effect.

Automatic Stabilizers

  • Unemployment insurance: Automatically increases during recessions
  • Progressive income taxes: Automatically adjusts tax burden
  • Welfare/SNAP/Medicaid: Enrollment rises during downturns

Deficits, Surpluses & Debt

  • Budget deficit: Spending > revenue. Financed by Treasury bonds.
  • Budget surplus: Revenue > spending
  • National debt: Accumulated deficits. US exceeds $36 trillion.
  • Crowding out: Government borrowing raises interest rates → private investment falls.

Lags in Fiscal Policy

  • Legislative lag: Congress must debate and pass — fiscal policy's biggest weakness
  • Implementation lag: Government spending takes time
Fiscal policy is more powerful but slower. Monetary policy is faster but less direct. Severe recessions use both together.
International Economics

Trade & Comparative Advantage

Comparative vs. Absolute Advantage

  • Absolute advantage: Producing more with the same resources
  • Comparative advantage: Lower opportunity cost — basis for all trade
Even if one country is better at everything, both gain from specializing in their comparative advantage.

Trade Barriers

  • Tariff: Tax on imports
  • Quota: Limit on quantity of imports
  • Embargo: Complete ban on trade
  • Subsidy: Government payment to domestic producers

Trade Agreements

  • WTO: Regulates international trade rules
  • USMCA: US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (replaced NAFTA)
  • Balance of Trade: Exports − Imports
International Economics

Exchange Rates

Exchange Rate Basics

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another.

Appreciation & Depreciation

  • Appreciation: Currency gains value → exports expensive → imports cheaper
  • Depreciation: Currency loses value → exports cheaper → imports expensive
A strong dollar = good for travelers/importers; bad for exporters.

Fixed vs. Floating

  • Fixed: Government pegs currency. Stability but less flexibility.
  • Floating: Market forces. Most major currencies float.

  • Stagflation risk is key concern for 2026

AI, Tech & Economy

  • AI impact on jobs debated: productivity gains vs. hiring slowdowns
  • CHIPS Act driving domestic semiconductor investment
