Microeconomics

Supply & Demand

⬆️ The Law of Demand

As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship), ceteris paribus. The demand curve slopes downward.

Demand Shifters (TRIBE):

  • Tastes & preferences
  • Related goods prices (substitutes & complements)
  • Income of buyers
  • Buyers (number of buyers in market)
  • Expectations of future prices

⬇️ The Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases (positive relationship). The supply curve slopes upward.

Supply Shifters (ROTTEN):

  • Resource costs (input prices)
  • Other goods' prices (opportunity cost)
  • Technology improvements
  • Taxes & subsidies
  • Expectations of sellers
  • Number of sellers

⚖️ Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium is where quantity demanded = quantity supplied. The price at this intersection is the equilibrium price (market-clearing price).

💡 Surplus: Price too high → Qs > Qd. Price falls back to equilibrium.
Shortage: Price too low → Qd > Qs. Price rises back to equilibrium.
Microeconomics

Elasticity

📐 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a price change.

PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % Change in Price
  • |PED| > 1 → Elastic (luxury goods, many substitutes)
  • |PED| < 1 → Inelastic (necessities, few substitutes)
  • |PED| = 1 → Unit elastic

📦 Determinants of Elasticity

  • Number of substitutes (more = more elastic)
  • Necessity vs. luxury (necessities = inelastic)
  • Time horizon (longer = more elastic)
  • Proportion of income spent (higher proportion = more elastic)

📊 Income Elasticity

YED = % Change in Demand ÷ % Change in Income
  • YED > 0 → Normal good (demand rises with income)
  • YED < 0 → Inferior good (demand falls with income)
Microeconomics

Market Structures

🏆 Perfect Competition

  • Many buyers and sellers
  • Identical (homogeneous) products
  • Free entry and exit
  • Price takers — firms have no pricing power
  • Long-run: zero economic profit

👑 Monopoly

  • Single seller controls the entire market
  • Unique product with no close substitutes
  • High barriers to entry
  • Price maker — sets price above competitive level
  • Results in deadweight loss

🤝 Oligopoly & Monopolistic Competition

  • Oligopoly: Few large firms, interdependent pricing, high barriers (e.g., airlines, telecom)
  • Monopolistic competition: Many firms, differentiated products, easy entry/exit (e.g., restaurants)
Microeconomics

Consumer Theory

😊 Utility

Utility is the satisfaction a consumer gets from a good or service.

  • Marginal Utility (MU): Additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional unit consumed gives less additional satisfaction

🔄 Substitution & Income Effects

  • Substitution effect: When price rises, consumers switch to cheaper substitutes
  • Income effect: When price rises, purchasing power falls → buy less

🏷️ Price Controls

  • Price ceiling: Maximum legal price (below equilibrium → shortage). E.g., rent control
  • Price floor: Minimum legal price (above equilibrium → surplus). E.g., minimum wage
💡 Remember: ceilings are below equilibrium (cause shortages), floors are above equilibrium (cause surpluses).
Macroeconomics

GDP & Economic Growth

📊 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced in a country in one year.

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • C = Consumer spending (largest component ~70%)
  • I = Investment (business spending on capital)
  • G = Government spending
  • X − M = Net exports (exports minus imports)

📈 Real vs. Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices (not adjusted for inflation)
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation — better measure of actual growth
Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator × 100

🔄 Business Cycle

The economy moves through phases: Expansion → Peak → Contraction/Recession → Trough → Recovery

💡 A recession is technically two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.
Macroeconomics

Unemployment

📉 Types of Unemployment

  • Frictional: Temporarily between jobs (job searching). Normal and healthy.
  • Structural: Skills mismatch — jobs exist but workers lack required skills
  • Cyclical: Due to economic downturn/recession. Demand-deficient.
  • Seasonal: Due to seasonal fluctuations (e.g., ski instructors in summer)

📐 Unemployment Rate

Unemployment Rate = (Unemployed ÷ Labor Force) × 100

Labor force = employed + unemployed (actively seeking work). Does NOT include discouraged workers.

🎯 Natural Rate of Unemployment

The natural rate (NAIRU) = frictional + structural unemployment. Typically around 4–5%. Cyclical unemployment is zero at full employment.

Macroeconomics

Inflation

💵 What is Inflation?

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level. Measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

Inflation Rate = [(CPI₂ − CPI₁) ÷ CPI₁] × 100

🔥 Types of Inflation

  • Demand-pull: "Too much money chasing too few goods" — driven by increased aggregate demand
  • Cost-push: Rising input/production costs push prices up (e.g., oil price shock)
  • Built-in: Wage-price spiral — workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices

⚖️ Inflation Effects

  • Hurts creditors (lenders) and helps debtors (borrowers)
  • Erodes purchasing power of fixed incomes
  • Deflation (falling prices) can be dangerous — delays spending
  • The Fed targets ~2% inflation
Macroeconomics

Monetary Policy

🏦 The Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve (Fed) is the central bank of the United States. It controls monetary policy to promote maximum employment and stable prices (dual mandate).

🔧 Tools of the Fed

  • Federal funds rate: Interest rate banks charge each other overnight — the Fed's main tool
  • Open market operations: Buying/selling government securities to adjust money supply
  • Reserve requirement: Minimum reserves banks must hold
  • Discount rate: Rate Fed charges banks for direct loans

➡️ Expansionary vs. Contractionary

  • Expansionary: Lower rates → increase money supply → stimulate economy (fight recession)
  • Contractionary: Raise rates → decrease money supply → slow economy (fight inflation)
💡 Quantitative easing (QE): Fed buys long-term bonds to inject money — used when rates near zero.
Macroeconomics

Fiscal Policy

🏛️ What is Fiscal Policy?

Government use of taxation and spending to influence the economy. Set by Congress and the President.

📈 Expansionary vs. Contractionary

  • Expansionary: Increase spending or cut taxes → boost aggregate demand (used in recessions)
  • Contractionary: Decrease spending or raise taxes → reduce aggregate demand (used against inflation)

⚡ Multiplier Effect & Automatic Stabilizers

  • Multiplier: $1 of government spending generates more than $1 in GDP (ripple effect)
  • Automatic stabilizers: Built-in policies that respond automatically — unemployment insurance, progressive taxes
💡 Fiscal policy has recognition, decision, and implementation lags, making it slower than monetary policy.
International Economics

Trade & Comparative Advantage

🌐 Comparative vs. Absolute Advantage

  • Absolute advantage: Producing more of a good with the same resources
  • Comparative advantage: Producing a good at lower opportunity cost — basis for all trade
💡 Even if one country is better at producing everything, both countries gain from specializing in their comparative advantage.

🚧 Trade Barriers

  • Tariff: Tax on imports — raises price, protects domestic producers, reduces trade volume
  • Quota: Limit on quantity of imports
  • Embargo: Complete ban on trade with a country
  • Subsidy: Government payment to domestic producers to boost competitiveness

📜 Trade Agreements & Organizations

  • WTO (World Trade Organization): Regulates international trade rules
  • USMCA: US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (replaced NAFTA)
  • Balance of Trade: Exports − Imports. Positive = trade surplus; Negative = trade deficit
International Economics

Exchange Rates

💱 Exchange Rate Basics

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. Set by supply and demand for currencies in the foreign exchange market.

📈 Currency Appreciation & Depreciation

  • Appreciation: Currency gains value → exports become more expensive → imports become cheaper
  • Depreciation: Currency loses value → exports become cheaper → imports become more expensive
💡 A strong dollar = good for American travelers abroad and importers; bad for American exporters.

🔄 Fixed vs. Floating Exchange Rates

  • Fixed: Government pegs currency to another (e.g., USD). Stability but less flexibility.
  • Floating: Determined by market forces. Most major currencies (USD, EUR, JPY) float.
Current Events

Key Topics 2024–25

📰 Federal Reserve & Inflation

  • The Fed raised rates aggressively in 2022–23 to combat post-pandemic inflation (reached ~9% CPI in 2022)
  • 2024: Fed began cutting rates as inflation cooled toward the 2% target
  • Key metric to know: PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) — the Fed's preferred inflation measure

💼 Labor Market

  • US unemployment rate stayed relatively low (~3.5–4%) through 2024
  • Labor force participation rate: % of working-age population working or looking for work
  • Wage growth and "tight" labor markets were major economic topics

🌐 Trade & Geopolitics

  • US-China trade tensions; tariffs and supply chain reshoring
  • Nearshoring/Friendshoring: Moving supply chains to nearby or allied countries
  • National debt levels and deficit spending debates

💻 Technology & Economy

  • AI and automation impact on labor markets (productivity vs. displacement debate)
  • Semiconductor industry investment (CHIPS Act)
  • Green energy transition economics
