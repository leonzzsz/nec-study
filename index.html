Microeconomics

Supply & Demand

⬆️ The Law of Demand

As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship), ceteris paribus. The demand curve slopes downward due to two effects:

  • Substitution effect: As the price of a good rises, consumers substitute cheaper alternatives
  • Income effect: A higher price reduces real purchasing power, so consumers buy less
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional unit consumed provides less satisfaction, so consumers only buy more at lower prices

Demand Shifters — TRIBE (shift the entire curve, not just movement along it):

  • Tastes & preferences — e.g., a health trend increases demand for organic food
  • Related goods — if complement price rises, demand falls; if substitute price rises, demand increases
  • Income — normal goods: demand rises with income; inferior goods: demand falls
  • Buyers — more consumers in a market shifts demand right
  • Expectations — if consumers expect higher future prices, they buy more now (shifts right)
💡 Key distinction: A change in price causes a movement along the demand curve (change in quantity demanded). A change in any other factor shifts the curve (change in demand).

⬇️ The Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases (positive relationship). The supply curve slopes upward because higher prices make production more profitable, incentivizing more output.

Supply Shifters — ROTTEN:

  • Resource/input costs — rising wages or raw material costs shift supply left (decrease supply)
  • Other goods' prices — if a related good becomes more profitable to produce, supply of this good falls
  • Technology — improvements lower production costs, shift supply right
  • Taxes & subsidies — taxes increase costs (shift left); subsidies decrease costs (shift right)
  • Expectations — if sellers expect higher future prices, they may withhold supply now
  • Number of sellers — more producers = greater market supply (shift right)

⚖️ Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where Qd = Qs — the market-clearing price. At this point, there is no tendency for price to change.

  • Surplus (excess supply): Price above equilibrium → Qs > Qd → downward pressure on price
  • Shortage (excess demand): Price below equilibrium → Qd > Qs → upward pressure on price
Supply & Demand — Market Equilibrium
Equilibrium Shift Rules
D↑ (right)→ P* rises, Q* rises
D↓ (left)→ P* falls, Q* falls
S↑ (right)→ P* falls, Q* rises
S↓ (left)→ P* rises, Q* falls
Both shiftOne of P* or Q* becomes indeterminate without magnitudes

🧮 Consumer & Producer Surplus

  • Consumer surplus: The difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Area above price, below demand curve.
  • Producer surplus: The difference between the price producers receive and their minimum acceptable price. Area below price, above supply curve.
  • Total surplus (social welfare): Consumer surplus + Producer surplus. Maximized at competitive equilibrium.
  • Deadweight loss: Loss of total surplus when output deviates from equilibrium quantity (caused by price controls, taxes, monopoly)
Consumer & Producer Surplus

🏷️ Price Controls

  • Price ceiling: A legally imposed maximum price set below equilibrium. Creates a persistent shortage. Example: rent control. Helps buyers in the short run but reduces quantity and quality over time.
  • Price floor: A legally imposed minimum price set above equilibrium. Creates a persistent surplus. Example: minimum wage creates unemployment (surplus of labor). Agricultural price floors create crop surpluses.
💡 Both price controls create deadweight loss and reduce market efficiency. Ceilings → shortages. Floors → surpluses.

💸 Taxes & Subsidies (Wedge Analysis)

  • A per-unit tax drives a wedge between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive. Quantity falls, deadweight loss is created.
  • Tax incidence: Who actually bears the burden depends on elasticity — the less elastic side bears more of the tax burden.
  • A subsidy works in reverse — lowers the price buyers pay, raises seller revenue, increases quantity above equilibrium (can also create inefficiency).
Tax burden on buyers = (Price buyers pay) − (Original equilibrium price)
Tax burden on sellers = (Original equilibrium price) − (Price sellers receive)

Elasticity

📐 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. Always use absolute value.

Price Elasticity of Demand
PED= |% ΔQd ÷ % ΔP|
Midpoint= [(Q₂−Q₁)/((Q₁+Q₂)/2)] ÷ [(P₂−P₁)/((P₁+P₂)/2)]
  • |PED| > 1 → Elastic: consumers are sensitive to price. % change in Qd > % change in P
  • |PED| < 1 → Inelastic: consumers are insensitive to price. % change in Qd < % change in P
  • |PED| = 1 → Unit elastic: % change in Qd = % change in P
  • |PED| = 0 → Perfectly inelastic: vertical demand curve (e.g., insulin)
  • |PED| = ∞ → Perfectly elastic: horizontal demand curve (price-taking firms in perfect competition)
Elastic vs. Inelastic Demand Curves

📦 Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand

  • Availability of substitutes: More substitutes = more elastic (e.g., Pepsi vs. a life-saving drug)
  • Necessity vs. luxury: Necessities (insulin, gas) tend to be inelastic; luxuries (vacations, jewelry) tend to be elastic
  • Time horizon: Demand becomes more elastic over time as consumers find alternatives (e.g., fuel efficiency cars in response to high gas prices)
  • Proportion of income: Goods that consume a large share of income tend to be more elastic (housing vs. salt)
  • Narrowness of definition: "Food" is inelastic; "McDonald's hamburgers" is elastic because many substitutes exist

💰 Elasticity & Total Revenue

Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity. Elasticity determines how TR responds to a price change:

  • Elastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↓ (Qd falls proportionally more than price rises)
  • Inelastic demand: Price ↑ → TR ↑ (Qd falls proportionally less than price rises)
  • Unit elastic: Price change has no effect on TR
💡 This is why drug companies and utilities can raise prices (inelastic) and still make more revenue, but airlines offering sales (elastic) increase revenue by lowering prices.

📊 Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

Price Elasticity of Supply
PES= % ΔQs ÷ % ΔP
PES > 1Elastic — producers ramp output easily
PES < 1Inelastic — hard to increase output quickly (e.g., beachfront land)
  • Key determinant: time — supply is more elastic in the long run as producers can adjust capacity

📈 Income & Cross-Price Elasticity

Income & Cross-Price Elasticity
YED= % ΔDemand ÷ % ΔIncome
XED= % ΔDemand for Good A ÷ % ΔPrice of Good B
YED > 0Normal good (demand ↑ with income)
YED < 0Inferior good (demand ↓ with income)
XED > 0Substitutes (Coke & Pepsi)
XED < 0Complements (cars & gas)

Market Structures

🏆 Perfect Competition

  • Many buyers and sellers; no single firm influences price
  • Identical (homogeneous) products — no differentiation
  • Perfect information for all participants
  • Free entry and exit in the long run
  • Firms are price takers — they face a perfectly elastic (horizontal) demand curve at the market price
  • Short run: Firms can earn economic profit, break even, or suffer losses
  • Long run: Entry/exit eliminates economic profit → firms earn zero economic profit (but still earn normal/accounting profit)
  • Produces at allocative efficiency (P = MC) and productive efficiency (P = min ATC)
Profit Maximization Rule (All Firms)
All firmsProduce where MR = MC
Perf. Comp.P = MR, so produce where P = MC
Economic π= (P − ATC) × Q

👑 Monopoly

  • Single seller — the firm IS the market; faces the downward-sloping market demand curve
  • Unique product with no close substitutes
  • Significant barriers to entry: patents, economies of scale (natural monopoly), control of key resources, government licensing
  • Price maker — chooses price by choosing output level on the demand curve
  • MR < P (to sell more, the monopolist must lower price on all units)
  • Produces where MR = MC, then charges the price from the demand curve above that output
  • Results in deadweight loss — underproduces relative to competitive outcome
  • NOT allocatively or productively efficient: P > MC and P > min ATC
Monopoly — Profit Maximization & Deadweight Loss
💡 Price discrimination: A monopolist charging different prices to different customers (e.g., airline seats, student discounts) can capture more consumer surplus and reduce deadweight loss.

🍕 Monopolistic Competition

  • Many firms selling differentiated products (perceived differences via branding, quality, location)
  • Low barriers to entry and exit
  • Each firm has some pricing power (slight downward-sloping demand), but faces strong competition
  • Short run: Can earn economic profit (attracts new entrants)
  • Long run: New entry drives profits to zero, like perfect competition
  • Excess capacity in long run — firms don't produce at minimum ATC (some productive inefficiency)
  • Examples: restaurants, clothing brands, hair salons

🤝 Oligopoly

  • Few large firms dominate the market; each firm has significant market power
  • High barriers to entry (capital costs, brand loyalty, economies of scale)
  • Interdependence: Each firm's decisions affect and are affected by competitors — must anticipate rivals' responses
  • Collusion & cartels: Firms may agree to fix prices and act like a monopoly (e.g., OPEC). Illegal in the US (antitrust law).
  • Game theory: Firms use strategic thinking; the prisoner's dilemma explains why cartels tend to break down
  • Examples: airlines, major tech firms, auto manufacturers, oil companies

📊 Market Structure Comparison

Market Structure Comparison
Perf. Comp.Many firms | Homogeneous | Price taker | P=MC | Zero LR profit
Mono. Comp.Many firms | Differentiated | Some power | P>MC | Zero LR profit
OligopolyFew firms | Diff/Homo | Price maker | P>MC | Possible LR profit
MonopolyOne firm | Unique | Price maker | P>MC | Possible LR profit

Consumer Theory & Market Failures

😊 Utility & Marginal Analysis

Utility is the satisfaction or benefit a consumer receives from consuming a good or service.

  • Total Utility (TU): Total satisfaction from all units consumed
  • Marginal Utility (MU): Additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit. MU = ΔTU / ΔQ
  • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility: As consumption increases, each additional unit provides less additional utility. Explains why demand curves slope downward.
Utility-maximizing rule: MU₁/P₁ = MU₂/P₂ (allocate spending so last dollar spent on each good gives equal marginal utility)

💼 Costs of Production

  • Explicit costs: Direct monetary payments (wages, rent, materials)
  • Implicit costs: Opportunity costs of using owned resources (e.g., foregone salary of owner)
  • Economic profit = Total Revenue − (Explicit + Implicit costs)
  • Accounting profit = Total Revenue − Explicit costs only
  • Normal profit: Zero economic profit — the firm is covering all opportunity costs, just enough to stay in business
Cost Formulas
TC= Fixed Costs (FC) + Variable Costs (VC)
MC= ΔTC ÷ ΔQ (change in total cost per extra unit)
ATC= TC ÷ Q (average total cost)
AFC= FC ÷ Q (falls as Q rises)
AVC= VC ÷ Q
Econ. π= TR − (Explicit + Implicit costs)
💡 The MC curve intersects the ATC and AVC curves at their minimum points. When MC < ATC, ATC is falling. When MC > ATC, ATC is rising.

📏 Returns to Scale & Economies of Scale

  • Economies of scale: As output increases, long-run ATC falls (e.g., specialization, bulk purchasing). Reason large firms dominate some industries.
  • Diseconomies of scale: As output grows too large, long-run ATC rises (coordination problems, bureaucracy)
  • Constant returns to scale: Long-run ATC stays flat as output grows
  • Natural monopoly: Industry where one firm can serve the entire market at lower cost than multiple firms due to very large economies of scale (e.g., utilities, water)

🌍 Externalities & Market Failures

A market failure occurs when the free market does not allocate resources efficiently. Major causes:

  • Negative externality: A cost imposed on a third party not involved in the transaction (e.g., pollution). Market overproduces — MSC > MPC. Government fix: tax (Pigouvian tax) or regulation.
  • Positive externality: A benefit received by a third party (e.g., education, vaccination). Market underproduces — MSB > MPB. Government fix: subsidy or public provision.
  • Public goods: Non-excludable and non-rival (e.g., national defense, lighthouses). Subject to free-rider problem → private market underprovides.
  • Asymmetric information: When one party has more information than another (e.g., used cars — "lemons problem", health insurance — adverse selection)
Externality Rules
Social opt.MSB = MSC (Marginal Social Benefit = Marginal Social Cost)
Neg. ext.MPC < MSC → market overproduces → fix with tax
Pos. ext.MPB < MSB → market underproduces → fix with subsidy
Macroeconomics

GDP & Economic Growth

📊 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period.

  • Only final goods are counted — intermediate goods are excluded to avoid double counting
  • Only new production counts — resale of used goods is excluded
  • Only goods produced within the country (GNP counts by nationality of producer)
  • Transfer payments (Social Security, welfare) are NOT included — no production occurs
GDP Expenditure Approach
GDP= C + I + G + (X − M)
CConsumer spending (~70% of US GDP)
IGross private investment (equipment, construction, inventory)
GGovernment spending on goods/services (NOT transfers)
X − MNet exports (exports minus imports)

📈 Real vs. Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current year prices — inflated by price increases over time
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation using a base year — measures actual changes in output
  • GDP Deflator: Broad price index used to convert nominal → real
Real GDP & Growth Rate
Real GDP= (Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator) × 100
Growth %= [(Real GDP₂ − Real GDP₁) ÷ Real GDP₁] × 100
💡 If nominal GDP grows 5% but inflation is 3%, real GDP only grew 2%. Always use real GDP to compare economic performance over time.

🔄 Business Cycle

The economy naturally fluctuates through recurring phases:

  • Expansion: Rising real GDP, falling unemployment, rising incomes and consumer confidence
  • Peak: Maximum output; often accompanied by high inflation pressure
  • Contraction/Recession: Falling real GDP for 2+ consecutive quarters; rising unemployment, falling spending
  • Trough: Lowest point of economic output
  • Recovery/Expansion: GDP begins rising again; unemployment gradually falls
The Business Cycle
💡 Leading indicators predict future activity (stock prices, building permits, consumer confidence). Lagging indicators confirm trends after the fact (unemployment rate, CPI).

📐 Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply (AD-AS)

The AD-AS model explains the economy's price level and real output simultaneously.

  • Aggregate Demand (AD): Total spending in the economy (C+I+G+NX). Slopes downward due to wealth effect, interest rate effect, and net exports effect.
  • Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS): Slopes upward — higher prices incentivize more production in the short run when input prices are fixed.
  • Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS): Vertical at the full-employment (potential) output level — in the long run, output depends only on resources and technology, not price level.
  • Output gap: Difference between actual and potential GDP. Negative gap = recession; Positive gap = inflationary pressure.
AD-AS Model — Short Run & Long Run
AD-AS Shift Rules
AD rightC↑, I↑, G↑, NX↑ → higher output + higher prices (SR)
SRAS leftInput costs rise (e.g., oil) → stagflation
LRAS rightMore resources / better tech → long-run growth

📉 Recessionary & Inflationary Gaps

  • Recessionary gap: Actual output < potential output. Unemployment above natural rate. AD has shifted left. Fix: expansionary policy (cut rates, increase spending).
  • Inflationary gap: Actual output > potential output. Unemployment below natural rate. Economy is "overheating." Fix: contractionary policy (raise rates, cut spending).
  • In the long run, wages adjust: in a recession, wages fall → SRAS shifts right → economy self-corrects back to potential output (but this is slow and painful)
Macroeconomics

Unemployment

📉 Types of Unemployment

  • Frictional: Short-term unemployment from transitioning between jobs. Voluntary and normal — reflects healthy job matching. Always exists even at "full employment."
  • Structural: Long-term mismatch between workers' skills and available jobs. Caused by technological change, industry shifts, globalization. Hardest to fix — requires retraining.
  • Cyclical: Caused by economic downturns — demand for workers falls during recessions. This is the "bad" type policymakers target with fiscal and monetary policy.
  • Seasonal: Regular, predictable changes due to time of year (farm workers, ski instructors, retail holiday hiring).
💡 Full employment does NOT mean 0% unemployment — it means cyclical unemployment = 0. Frictional and structural unemployment always exist.

📐 Measuring Unemployment

Unemployment Formulas
U-rate= (# Unemployed ÷ Labor Force) × 100
Labor Force= Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking)
LFPR= (Labor Force ÷ Working-Age Population) × 100
  • Who is NOT counted as unemployed: People not actively looking for work, retirees, students, stay-at-home parents
  • Discouraged workers: People who have given up looking for work — NOT counted in the official unemployment rate, which causes the rate to understate true unemployment
  • Underemployment: Workers in part-time jobs who want full-time work, or workers in jobs below their skill level — also not captured in the headline rate
  • The BLS publishes multiple measures: U-3 (official rate) through U-6 (broadest, includes discouraged + underemployed)

🎯 Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU)

The Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU) = frictional + structural unemployment. Represents "full employment" for an economy.

  • Typically estimated at 4–5% for the US economy
  • When actual unemployment < NAIRU: labor market is "tight," wages and inflation tend to rise
  • When actual unemployment > NAIRU: recessionary gap exists, output below potential
  • The NAIRU is not fixed — it can shift with demographics, skills, technology, labor market policies

📊 Unemployment & the Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve shows the short-run trade-off between unemployment and inflation.

  • Short-run: Inverse relationship — lower unemployment tends to accompany higher inflation (tight labor markets → wage pressure → higher prices)
  • Long-run: The Phillips Curve is vertical at the natural rate — there is no long-run trade-off. Expansionary policy that reduces unemployment below NAIRU eventually just causes higher inflation.
  • Stagflation (1970s) broke the short-run Phillips Curve relationship — both unemployment AND inflation were high simultaneously, caused by supply-side oil shocks.

Inflation

💵 What is Inflation?

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services, which erodes the purchasing power of money.

Inflation Formulas
Inflation= [(CPI₂ − CPI₁) ÷ CPI₁] × 100
CPI= (Cost of basket current year ÷ Cost in base year) × 100
  • CPI (Consumer Price Index): Tracks price changes of a fixed "basket" of consumer goods — the most common inflation measure
  • PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures): The Fed's preferred measure — broader and adjusts for substitution between goods
  • Core inflation: CPI or PCE excluding food and energy prices (more volatile) — gives a cleaner signal of underlying inflation trends
  • PPI (Producer Price Index): Measures inflation from the seller's perspective — often a leading indicator of future CPI

🔥 Causes of Inflation

  • Demand-pull inflation: Aggregate demand rises faster than productive capacity — "too much money chasing too few goods." Occurs during booms, high government spending, or rapid money supply growth. AD shifts right → price level rises.
  • Cost-push inflation: A negative supply shock raises production costs, pushing SRAS left — output falls AND prices rise simultaneously (stagflation). Examples: 1970s oil embargos, pandemic supply chain disruptions.
  • Built-in / Wage-price spiral: Workers expect inflation → demand higher wages → firms raise prices → inflation continues. Becomes self-fulfilling if expectations are not anchored.
  • Quantity theory of money: MV = PQ. If money supply (M) grows faster than real output (Q), prices (P) must rise.

⚖️ Effects of Inflation

  • Redistribution: Hurts creditors (lenders) — they get repaid in dollars worth less. Helps debtors (borrowers) — they repay with cheaper dollars.
  • Fixed-income earners: Harmed — their nominal income stays constant while prices rise, so real purchasing power falls
  • Real vs. nominal interest rates: Real rate = Nominal rate − Inflation rate (Fisher equation). High inflation erodes the real return on savings.
  • Menu costs: Resources wasted on constantly updating prices
  • Shoe-leather costs: People make more trips to the bank to hold less cash during high inflation
Fisher Equation
Real Rate= Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

📉 Deflation & Hyperinflation

  • Deflation: Falling price level — sounds good but is dangerous. Consumers delay purchases expecting lower future prices → spending collapses → recession deepens. Example: Japan's "Lost Decade."
  • Disinflation: Inflation is still positive but slowing (not the same as deflation)
  • Hyperinflation: Extremely rapid inflation (>50%/month) — destroys the value of currency, economic chaos. Examples: Zimbabwe 2008, Germany 1923.
  • The Fed targets 2% inflation — low and stable enough to avoid deflation risk while not distorting economic decisions

Monetary Policy

🏦 The Federal Reserve System

The Federal Reserve (Fed) is the central bank of the United States, established in 1913. It is independent of the government to avoid political pressure.

  • Structure: Board of Governors (7 members) + 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks. The FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee) sets monetary policy — meets 8 times per year.
  • Dual mandate: Maximum employment AND stable prices (2% inflation target)
  • Chair: Jerome Powell (term expires May 2026)
  • The Fed acts as "lender of last resort" — provides emergency liquidity to banks during crises

🔧 Tools of Monetary Policy

  • Federal funds rate: The interest rate at which banks lend reserves to each other overnight. The Fed's primary tool — targets a range (e.g., 3.50–3.75% as of early 2026). Changes ripple through all other interest rates in the economy.
  • Open Market Operations (OMO): The Fed buys or sells Treasury securities on the open market.
    • Buy bonds → inject money into banking system → money supply ↑ → rates ↓ (expansionary)
    • Sell bonds → drain money from banking system → money supply ↓ → rates ↑ (contractionary)
  • Discount rate: Interest rate the Fed charges banks for direct borrowing at the "discount window." Higher than the fed funds rate — signal of last resort lending.
  • Reserve requirement: Minimum fraction of deposits banks must hold as reserves. Rarely used in modern policy — set to 0% since 2020.
  • Interest on Reserves (IOER/IOR): Fed pays interest on bank reserves — an important modern tool for controlling the floor of the fed funds rate.

➡️ Expansionary vs. Contractionary Monetary Policy

  • Expansionary ("easy money"): Fed lowers fed funds rate → borrowing becomes cheaper → investment and consumption rise → AD shifts right → output and employment increase. Used to fight recessions.
  • Contractionary ("tight money"): Fed raises fed funds rate → borrowing more expensive → investment and consumption fall → AD shifts left → inflation cools. Used to fight inflation.
Monetary Policy Transmission
Expansionary↓ Fed rate → ↓ interest rates → ↑ I & C → ↑ AD → ↑ GDP, ↓ unemployment
Contractionary↑ Fed rate → ↑ interest rates → ↓ I & C → ↓ AD → ↓ inflation

💡 Money Supply & the Money Multiplier

  • Money supply (M1): Currency in circulation + demand deposits (checking accounts)
  • Money supply (M2): M1 + savings accounts + small time deposits + money market funds
  • Money multiplier: An initial deposit gets re-lent through the banking system, multiplying the money supply
Money Multiplier
Multiplier= 1 ÷ Reserve Requirement
ΔMoney Supply= Initial Deposit × Money Multiplier
Example10% reserve req. → multiplier = 10 → $1,000 deposit = $10,000 created
💡 Quantitative Easing (QE): When rates hit near-zero and conventional policy is exhausted, the Fed buys longer-term assets (mortgage-backed securities, long-term Treasuries) to push down long-term rates. Used aggressively in 2008–2009 and 2020.

⏱️ Lags in Monetary Policy

  • Recognition lag: Time to identify that a problem exists
  • Implementation lag: Monetary policy is relatively fast — the FOMC can act at any of its 8 annual meetings (or emergency meetings)
  • Impact lag: 6–18 months for rate changes to fully work through the economy — the Fed must act on forecasts, not current data

Fiscal Policy

🏛️ What is Fiscal Policy?

Fiscal policy is the government's use of taxation and spending to influence macroeconomic conditions. Unlike monetary policy (controlled by the Fed), fiscal policy is set by Congress and the President.

  • Expansionary fiscal policy: Increase government spending (G↑) or cut taxes (T↓) → boosts aggregate demand → used during recessions
  • Contractionary fiscal policy: Decrease spending (G↓) or raise taxes (T↑) → reduces aggregate demand → used to fight inflation
Fiscal Policy Effects on AD
ExpansionaryG↑ or T↓ → C↑ or G↑ → AD shifts right → GDP↑, unemployment↓
ContractionaryG↓ or T↑ → C↓ or G↓ → AD shifts left → inflation↓

⚡ The Spending Multiplier

An initial change in government spending creates a chain reaction of spending throughout the economy — the total impact on GDP is larger than the initial injection.

Spending Multiplier
Multiplier= 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ MPS
MPCMarginal Propensity to Consume — fraction of extra income spent
MPSMarginal Propensity to Save — fraction of extra income saved
IdentityMPC + MPS = 1
ExampleMPC = 0.8 → Multiplier = 5 → $100B spending = $500B GDP impact
Tax mult.= −MPC ÷ MPS (smaller than spending multiplier)
  • Tax multiplier is smaller than spending multiplier because some of a tax cut is saved, not spent
  • Tax multiplier = −MPC ÷ MPS (negative because tax cuts increase income)
💡 The higher the MPC, the larger the multiplier. Economies where people spend more of each dollar have stronger multiplier effects.

🔄 Automatic Stabilizers

Automatic stabilizers are built-in fiscal mechanisms that cushion economic fluctuations without requiring new legislation — they respond automatically to economic conditions.

  • Unemployment insurance: Payments automatically increase during recessions (when unemployment rises), sustaining consumer spending
  • Progressive income taxes: During booms, higher incomes are taxed at higher rates → government collects more → automatically cools demand. During recessions, incomes fall → tax burden automatically decreases.
  • Welfare/SNAP/Medicaid: Enrollment automatically rises during downturns, injecting spending into the economy

💰 Budget Deficits, Surpluses & National Debt

  • Budget deficit: Government spending > tax revenue in a given year. Financed by issuing Treasury bonds.
  • Budget surplus: Tax revenue > government spending
  • National debt: The accumulation of all past budget deficits (minus surpluses). The US national debt exceeds $36 trillion.
  • Crowding out: When government borrows heavily, it competes with private borrowers → drives up interest rates → private investment falls. This partially offsets the stimulus effect of expansionary fiscal policy.
  • Debt-to-GDP ratio: A key indicator of fiscal sustainability. US ratio has risen significantly since 2008.

⏱️ Lags in Fiscal Policy

  • Recognition lag: Time to identify the economic problem
  • Decision/Legislative lag: Congress must debate and pass legislation — can take months or years. This is fiscal policy's biggest weakness vs. monetary policy.
  • Implementation lag: Government must actually spend the money — infrastructure projects take years
  • Impact lag: Effects ripple through the economy slowly
💡 Fiscal policy is more powerful but slower than monetary policy. Monetary policy is faster but less direct. In a severe recession, both are often used together.
International Economics

Trade & Comparative Advantage

🌐 Comparative vs. Absolute Advantage

  • Absolute advantage: Producing more of a good with the same resources
  • Comparative advantage: Producing a good at lower opportunity cost — basis for all trade
💡 Even if one country is better at producing everything, both countries gain from specializing in their comparative advantage.

🚧 Trade Barriers

  • Tariff: Tax on imports — raises price, protects domestic producers, reduces trade volume
  • Quota: Limit on quantity of imports
  • Embargo: Complete ban on trade with a country
  • Subsidy: Government payment to domestic producers to boost competitiveness

📜 Trade Agreements & Organizations

  • WTO (World Trade Organization): Regulates international trade rules
  • USMCA: US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (replaced NAFTA)
  • Balance of Trade: Exports − Imports. Positive = trade surplus; Negative = trade deficit
International Economics

Exchange Rates

💱 Exchange Rate Basics

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. Set by supply and demand for currencies in the foreign exchange market.

📈 Currency Appreciation & Depreciation

  • Appreciation: Currency gains value → exports become more expensive → imports become cheaper
  • Depreciation: Currency loses value → exports become cheaper → imports become more expensive
💡 A strong dollar = good for American travelers abroad and importers; bad for American exporters.

🔄 Fixed vs. Floating Exchange Rates

  • Fixed: Government pegs currency to another (e.g., USD). Stability but less flexibility.
  • Floating: Determined by market forces. Most major currencies (USD, EUR, JPY) float.

Key Topics 2025–26

📰 Federal Reserve & Inflation

  • After raising rates to a peak of ~5.25–5.5% in 2023, the Fed began cutting in late 2024 — by December 2025 the federal funds rate was cut to 3.50–3.75% (a total of 175 basis points in cuts since Sept 2024)
  • Inflation (CPI) came in at ~2.8% in 2025 — still above the Fed's 2% target, largely driven by tariff-related price pressures
  • The Fed's preferred measure is PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) — running ~2.8% heading into 2026
  • The Fed paused rate cuts in early 2026 due to uncertainty from tariffs, immigration policy, and a government shutdown that disrupted economic data
  • Jerome Powell's term as Fed Chair expires May 2026 — a new chair appointment could shift Fed messaging

💼 Labor Market

  • Unemployment rose from ~4.1% in early 2025 to ~4.4–4.6% by end of 2025 — a softening labor market
  • Job growth slowed significantly in 2025 — one of the weakest years for payroll gains since the 2009 financial crisis
  • A sharp decline in immigration reduced labor supply, contributing to slower workforce growth
  • Labor force participation rate: % of working-age population working or actively seeking work
  • Wage growth softened but still outpaced inflation by ~1%, keeping real wages positive
  • The labor market entered a "low-hire, low-fire" equilibrium — few layoffs but also fewer new jobs

🌐 Trade, Tariffs & Geopolitics

  • Tariffs were a dominant economic story in 2025 — sweeping new tariffs raised costs for importers and consumers
  • "Liberation Day" tariff announcements caused major business and market uncertainty in 2025
  • Tariff pass-through to consumers exceeded 50% — meaning households are paying meaningfully higher prices
  • USMCA (US-Mexico-Canada Agreement) is under renegotiation pressure; trade relations with Canada and Mexico complicated by tariffs
  • US-China tensions continued — tech export restrictions, AI competition, and an "arms race" in quantum computing
  • Nearshoring: Companies moving supply chains closer to home to reduce tariff exposure

📊 GDP & Economic Growth

  • US real GDP grew approximately 1.8–2% in 2025 — solid but slower than prior years
  • A 43-day government shutdown in late 2025 disrupted economic activity and data collection
  • Growth in 2026 is projected around 1.9–2.2% — supported by fiscal stimulus but held back by tariffs and weaker consumer spending
  • Stagflation risk is a key concern for 2026: inflation remains above target while unemployment is rising — historically hard for the Fed to address simultaneously

💻 AI, Technology & the Economy

  • AI investment surged — hyperscaler (big tech) capital spending on data centers rose ~69% in 2025 and is projected up another ~33% in 2026
  • AI's impact on jobs is debated: productivity gains vs. potential hiring slowdowns as CEOs gain confidence to reduce headcount
  • AI and quantum computing are a central front in US-China economic and tech competition
  • The CHIPS Act continues to drive domestic semiconductor manufacturing investment
💡 For NEC prep, follow The Wall Street Journal, the Federal Reserve website (federalreserve.gov), and the Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov) for the latest data.
